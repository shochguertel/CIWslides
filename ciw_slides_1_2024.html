Demand/Supply & Equilibrium

  • Demand-Supply Diagram
  • Demand
  • Supply
  • Equilibrium
  • Surplus
  • Notes
  • quantities demanded and supplied are functions of the price: \(D(p), S(p)\)

Observe!

Price is on the vertical, quantity on the horizontal axis!

  • equilibrium: \(p^\star: D(p^\star) = S(p^\star) \equiv Q^\star\)
  • demand is based on choices of ‘how to spend it and on what’:
  • rational consumers individually maximize their own satisfaction/utility
  • subject to available income, and given prices (budget constraint)
    • individual demand, per good
  • aggregate across all individuals, per good: market demand
    • for given price, add up the quantities (‘horizontal’ aggregation)
  • supply is based on choices of ‘what & how much to make’:
  • rational firms individually maximize their own profit
  • subject to available technology (production function), and given prices
    • individual supply, per good
  • aggregate across all individual firms, per good: market supply
    • for given price, add up the quantities (‘horizontal’ aggregation)
  • per good/market: market clearing implies quantity demanded equals quantity supplied
    • price determination per good
    • feedback into individual decisions
  • think of “the market” as a system of many markets of individual goods and inputs
    • considering a single market in isolation, we talk of partial equilibrium analysis
    • considering many markets simultaneously, we may talk of general equilibrium analysis
    • in this course, we may consider two markets at the same time
  • an equilibrium then is a set of prices leading to simultaneous market clearing of all markets in the system
  • total economic surplus is a first measure of welfare generated in the market
  • distinguish
    • consumer surplus (area between market demand and equilibrium price)
    • producer surplus (area between equilibrium price and market supply)
  • consumers also supply capital (savings) and labor to the market
    • consumers’ incomes are derived from their saving and working choices
    • this will make incomes endogenous to the analysis
  • firms also demand (financial and physical) capital and labor on the market
  • most models are static, things happen instantaneously, and at the same time

Utility Function

  • Utility Function
  • Ind’ Curve Maps
  • Ordinality and Incomparability
  • Notes
  • a utility function is a mathematical model for consumer preferences
  • with two goods, \(x\) and \(y\), we write \[u(x,y)\]
    • this is a three-dimensional object (two inputs, one output)
  • simple examples: \[u = x + y \qquad \text{or} \qquad u = x \cdot y\]
  • ICs are the 2D level curves of the 3D function \(u(x,y)\)
  • they can be obtained by horizontally ‘slicing’ the 3D function, keeping contant a vertical level of \(u\)
  • at any chosen level of \(u\), there is one indifference curve (IC)
  • slicing many times, gives many ICs (IC map)

  • level of utility has no intrinsic meaning, except a higher number indicates a higher level of satisfaction or valuation: more is better
  • this is an ordinal concept (order or ranking matters, not degree)
  • from a utility number, we cannot say anything about the absolute satisfaction or valuation
  • also, we cannot compare utilities between individuals
    • two consumers could use different scales to measure their utility

We shall demonstrate ordinality in class

  • utility functions and indifference curves contain the same information, but \(u()\) is efficient mathematical notation, hence convenient
  • ordinality and incomparability will be dropped when we analyze economic inequality and social choice
  • preferences (utility functions, indifference curves) are assumed to be stable between choice situations that we compare
    • indifference curves never move in our models
    • often enough, different indifference curves (different utility levels) will be chosen when circumstances change
  • utility focuses on the valuation of measurable quantities (consumption bundles)
    • utility \(\nRightarrow\) happiness

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)

  • Graph
  • Underlying Math\(^\star\)
  • Math Examples\(^\star\)
  • Notes
  • MRS is a fundamental concept to characterize consumer choice

MRS

  • tells how much a consumer is willing to give up in terms of units of \(y\) in return for getting an (infinitesimally small) unit of \(x\)
  • hence, measures willingness to give up, or willingness to pay
  • MRS (a postive number) equals (the negative of) the slope of the indifference curve

  • how does the \(u\)-value of \(u(x,y)\) change along an IC when we vary \(x\) and \(y\)?

  • the answer is: along an IC the change in \(u\), \(\text d u\), will be zero

  • using the formula for a total differential we can decompose this ‘zero change’ into changes due to either \(x\) or \(y\): \[ \text{d}u = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \text{d}x + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \text{d}y =0 \]

  • rearrangement (using simple algebra) leads to \[ \frac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = -\color{blue}{\frac{{\partial u}/{\partial x}}{{\partial u}/{\partial y}}} \]

  • the slope of the IC, \({\text{d}y}/{\text{d}x}\), equals the negative of the MRS

\(u(x,y) = \ln(x)+\ln(y)\): \[MRS = \frac{\frac{1}{x}}{\frac{1}{y}}=\frac{y}{x},\]

\(u(x,y) = \sqrt{x} \cdot \sqrt{y}\): \[MRS = \frac{\frac{\sqrt{y}}{2\sqrt{x}}}{\frac{\sqrt{x}}{2\sqrt{y}}}=\frac{y}{x}.\]

  • MRS is a local concept: along a given IC, MRS typically changes with \(x\) and \(y\)
    • there are exceptions (which ones?) Hint: play with the IC maps
  • different utility functions can have the same MRS (see tab Math Examples)
    • this is because different utility functions may differ in the 3rd but not in the other dimensions (ordinal concept)

1.2 Constraints & Optimization

Budget Constraint

  • consumers have a fixed income \(m\) and they face prices \(p_x\) and \(p_y\)

  • we call \(p_x\cdot x+p_y\cdot y\) expenditure

  • expenditure cannot exceed income \(p_x\cdot x+p_y\cdot y\le m\)

  • under ‘more-is-better’ consumers spend it all: budget constraint (BC) \[y = \frac{m}{p_y}-\frac{p_x}{p_y}\cdot x\]

  • consumers then maximize utility subject to the BC

  • in graph:

    • intersection points of BC with axes are \(\frac{m}{p_x}\) and \(\frac{m}{p_y}\)
    • slope is \(\displaystyle \frac{p_x}{p_y}\)

Two Ways to Maximize Utility

Substitute Out \(y\) using BC

Via MRS = Slope of BC

Notation: we write \(m\) for money income, and budget constraint (BC): \(m=p_x\cdot x+p_y\cdot y\) (income equals expenditure)

How to obtain the pictures will be demonstrated for class audience only.

Two Ways to Maximize Utility\(^\star\)

Substitute Out \(y\) using BC

  • we have 3 variables: \(x\), \(y\) and \(u\)
  • first get rid of one of them, for instance \(y\)
  • we use the budget constraint \[\quad\underbrace{\color{red}{y}=\frac{m}{p_y}-\frac{p_x}{p_y}\cdot x}_{\color{red}{\downarrow}}\]
  • and substitute into \(u(x,\color{red}{y})\)
  • then, we do simple maximization: \(\max_x u(x)\)

Via MRS = Slope of BC

  • we use the marginal condition for maximization \[\frac{MU_x(x,y)}{p_x} = \frac{MU_y(x,y)}{p_y} \quad \text{or} \quad MRS(x,y)=\frac{p_x}{p_y}\]
  • since there are (infinitely) many pairs of \((x,y)\) where this holds, we still need to combine with budget constraint \[\underbrace{\color{red}{y}=\frac{m}{p_y}-\frac{p_x}{p_y}\cdot x}_{\color{red}{\downarrow}}\]
  • and substitute into \(MRS(x,\color{red}{y})\)
  • then, we use algebra to solve for \(x\)

Which one’s easier? Depends on functional form.

Demand Diagram

Recall that economists write \(x = D(p_x)\) while putting \(p_x\) on the vertical and \(x\) on the horizontal axis.

Deriving demand will be demonstrated exclusively live in class

  • In a two-goods model, it is more accurate to write \[ \begin{split} x &= D_x(p_x,p_y,m) \\ y &= D_y(p_x,p_y,m) \\ \end{split} \] making clear that all quantities can depend on all prices and incomes
  • for the square-root Cobb Douglas utility, it is easy to show that \[ \begin{split} D_x(p_x,p_y,m) &= m/(2p_x)\\ D_y(p_x,p_y,m) &= m/(2p_y) \end{split} \] i.e., demands do not depend directly on the price of the other good.

Price Changes: Income and Substitution Effects

  • Effect of a Price Change
  • Graph
  • There are two effects on \(x\) from an increase in \(p_x\)

  1. consumers have lower purchasing power, real incomes decrease

  2. \(x\) becomes relatively more expensive compared to other goods, so consumers might substitute away and allocate their money differently

    1. is called income effect,
    2. is called substitution effect.
  • Income and substitution effects may go in the same, or in the opposite direction.
  • shape of indifference curves and/or MRS matters a lot
  • A is observed before-price change
  • B is observed after-price change
  • C is ‘virtual’ point: choice that would’ve been made had the consumer been fully utility-compensated for the income effect (drop in purchasing power)
    • utility level / IC is the same in A and C
  • A\(\to\)B is total effect
    • A\(\to\)C is substitution effect (along IC)
    • C\(\to\)B is income effect (between ICs)

Measuring Consumer Welfare

  • Compensating Variation
  • Graph
  • measure welfare changes expressed in money terms
  • renormalize \(p_y\equiv 1\), always
    • vertical intercept (on \(y\)-axis) then equals \(m\)
  • analysis of income/substitution effects: increase in \(p_x\) associated with a downward shift in real income between points A and B
  • conversely, need to make compensation payment to shift budget line from B to C in order to fully restore utility
  • this compensation payment to restore utility to its original value is called a compensating variation, CV
  • we do the same as with the income effect, but re-focus on the income change itself on the vertical axis
  • denote the budget before and after the price change by \(m_0\) and \(m_1\), then \(\Delta_m = m_0-m_1 \lt 0\) is the money-metric change in welfare that the consumer experiences from the price change, unless it is compensated with actually making sure the consumer would get \(m_1\)
  • when price goes up, welfare goes down

Conclusion

Summary

  • Choices are determined by
    • preferences
    • restrictions
  • Preferences can be illustrated using indifference curves, which in turn are derived from a number of axioms
  • Utility functions are a mathematical convenience tool to represent preferences, with ICs as their level curves
  • Utility maximization subject to a budget constraint leads to demand for goods
  • The demand function shows quantity demanded in dependence of prices and incomes
  • Prices and incomes can be influenced by policy (e.g., taxes and transfers)
  • Price variation can be used to derive individual welfare measures
  • The compensating variation is one such measure, the change in consumer surplus is another (the former is, the latter is not, purged of an income effect)
