1 Single Consumer

Single Consumer: Endowment

  • Budget Constraint
  • Graph
  • consumers may be equipped with goods that they can sell: endowment
  • make two assumptions:
    1. those goods are the same goods consumers could also buy
    2. prices for buying or selling are the same
    • picture changes, but analytical model unchanged
  • denote endowment quantities \(x^e\) and \(y^e\): these are parameters
  • BC changes to \[ \begin{split} \overbrace{p_x\cdot x+p_y\cdot y}^{\text{value of purchases}} &= \overbrace{m+p_x\cdot x^e+p_y\cdot y^e}^{\text{value of income and phys. endowment}} \\ \underbrace{p_x\cdot (x-x^e)+p_y\cdot (y-y^e)}_{\text{net purchase}} &= m. \end{split} \]
  • consumer will sell some of the good she has plenty of (compared to the desired optimal allocation)
  • consumer will buy more of the good she has little of

Observe!

You can shift the endowment point along the BC. A shift implies, however, adjusting the money income accordingly. Endowment and money income are perfect substitutes in the graph. Holding \(m\) constant would imply the entire BC to shift with the endowment.

2 Two Consumers

2.1 Edgeworth and Pareto

Exchange & Consumption Edgeworth Box

  • Exchange and Endowments
  • Edgeworth Box
  • Pareto Efficiency
  • we now consider two consumers \(A\) and \(B\) and two goods \(x\) and \(y\)
  • each consumer has their own utility function \[ u_A(x_A,y_A)\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad u_B(x_B,y_B) \]
  • there are neither prices nor income to start with
  • consumers have an endowment each: \(E_A=(x_A^e,y_A^e)\) and \(E_B=(x_B^e,y_B^e)\)
  • total resources of \(x\) and \(y\) available to any consumer are fixed at \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\)
    • whatever is not owned by \(A\), is owned by \(B\): \(E_B=(\bar x - x_A^e,\bar y - y_A^e)\)
  • consumers can exchange (swap) units of \(x\) and \(y\) with each other to improve their own utility

  • \(E\) is the endowment point: this is a double point with 4(!) coordinates, reflecting both \(E_A\) and \(E_B\)

  • Because of the restriction on overall resources, we draw two indifference curve maps for \(A\) and \(B\) into a single graph (IC map for \(B\) is flipped around)

  • read off all the units for \(B\) from right to left and from top to bottom, starting at the origin in the upper right corner at \(O_B\)

  • Utility for \(B\) improves going south-west.

Pareto Preferred

An allocation \({\cal A}\) is Pareto preferred to allocation \({\cal B}\) if by moving from \({\cal B}\) to \({\cal A}\) at least one agent is strictly better off, and no-one else loses (Pareto improvement). Named after Vilfredo Pareto, 1848–1923.

  • i.o.w.: Pareto Improvement = mutual gains from trade can be realized

Pareto Efficient

An allocation is Pareto efficient if there is no allocation which is Pareto preferred to it (Pareto superior)

  • i.o.w.: all possibilities for Pareto improvement are exhausted
  • Edgeworth box: \(MRS_A = MRS_B\) along contract curve

Intuition will be demonstrated exclusively in class.

Utility Possibilities Frontier (UPF)

  • Issue
  • Construction
  • (cont’d.)
  • 2D Utilities
  • 3D Utilities
  • Remark: Shape
  • we now consider the level of utility and ask: what is the highest level of utilities that individuals can achieve in a 2-person economy with given resources?
  • i.o.w.: how much utility can we get out of our stock of consumption goods, at most, if we are willing to redistribute between agents?
    • UPF shows the maximum attainable level of utility for all members of society
    • analysis is: given \(u_A\), what is \(\max u_B\)? (or converse)
  • for convenience, we consider utilities that are always \(\ge 0\) \(\to\) origin of graph at 0
  • we can keep ordinality

  • consider two consumers (\(A,B\)) between which a fixed amount \(\bar c\) of a single consumption good can be divided

  • a unit of the good can be consumed by \(A\) or by \(B\), but not by both at the same time: \(\qquad c_B = \bar c - c_A\)

    • increasing consumption (and, hence, utility) of \(A\) is associated with a decrease in consumption (and, hence, utility) of \(B\)

  • this linkage gives rise to the utility possibilities frontier

  • UPF shows how much utility \(A\) has to give up if a reallocation of the consumption good leads to an increase in utility of \(B\)

  • if the \(u^j\), \(j=A,B\) functions are ordinal, then UPF will be ordinal as well

  • all points on the UPF boundary are Pareto efficient, since utility of one person can only be improved upon at the expense of utility of another person

  • with 2 consumption goods, we can infer the UPF from the Edgeworth Box

  • then, UPF corresponds to Pareto efficient points on contract curve, i.e., we consider the levels of the indifference curves at the tangential points

  • example in next tab: drawn for utility functions with one argument \(u(c)\)

  • example in second next tab: drawn for utility functions with two arguments \(u(x,y)\)

    • UPF corresponds to contract curve in Edgeworth box

Get the hang of the UPF!

  • shift the green dot (3rd quadrant) to grasp how UPF comes about (1st quadrant)
  • shift the black dot (horizontal axis) to increase the total size of \(\bar c\)
  • shift the purple sliders to study the effect of the shape of \(u_A\)/\(u_B\) on UPF

  • UPF need not be nicely concave
  • the shape of the UPF may eventually play a role when society has to choose between allocations \(M\) and \(F\) that are both on the UPF, so both are Pareto efficient, but possibly evaluated differently, once distributional concerns are considered
  • non-concave functions are somewhat harder to capture mathematically (i.e., what our math can show has its limitations)
  • the slope of the UPF has no direct interpretation

First Theorem of Welfare Economics

  • Issue
  • Graph
  • Theorem
  • UPF

  • Edgeworth box shows which allocations are Pareto efficient, when individuals directly exchange physical quantities of goods, starting from \(E\)

  • market mechanism leads to indirect swapping, using buying and selling transactions, when participants trade anonymously ‘with the market’ at given prices \(p_x\) and \(p_y\)

  • can represent this through a price line, that has the same role as a budget constraint for an optimizing consumer

  • to clear the market (and bring quantities bought and sold in the aggregate into equilibrium), prices react endogenously, the price line changes slope

  • at initial (relative) prices (=slope of price line), total demand for \(x\) is smaller than the width of the box, \(\bar x\), whereas total demand for \(y\) is larger than the height of the box, \(\bar y\)
    • relative prices need to adjust, \(p_x/p_y\) needs to change

Clear the market!

Shift the moveable dot around to adjust the price ratio and find the market-clearing equilibrium price ratio. What can you then say about the relation of the \(MRS\) of both agents?

First Theorem of Welfare Economics

Under strict conditions, a competitive market equilibrium is Pareto efficient.

  • i.o.w.: market clearing \(\implies\) efficiency
  • starting from endowment \(E\), market interaction and market clearing lead to equilibrium market prices that make agents pick allocation \(M\)
  • 1st Theorem implies: \(M\) is on the UPF (between \(P\) and \(Q\)) and Pareto efficient

2.2 Social Welfare

Social Welfare Function (SWF)

  • SWF
  • Relevance
  • Shape
  • Equity
  • Graph
  • Math\(^\star\)
  • Remark
  • SWF: society’s `utility’ function
  • Bergson-Samuelson SWF \(W()\): purely based on individual utilities \[ W=W(u^A,u^B)\]
  • social decisions involve trade-off across groups or individuals
    • interpersonal comparability of utilities necessary when redistributing
  • similar to a utility function, SWF is a modeling device without any direct observable counterpart in the real world
  • in the same way as we can conceive a consumer of reacting to price and income changes as if she was maximizing a utility function, we can conceive of a policymaker as if their choices were guided by an SWF
  • this policymaker is often referred to as social planner
  • important is that SWF can be used to show how to balance off opposing interests against one another, that is, it is useful to model a trade-off between groups in society
  • economics offers little guidance as to what is the ‘correct’ shape of the SWF
  • normative and philosophical considerations may play a role
  • that information has to come from other ways at aggregating individual preferences to a social preference structure
  • we could conceive of a social choice mechanism that determines social preferences in a democracy being determined through an electoral voting process
  • in an autocracy, the preferences of a single individual (dictator) or of elite groups determine what to choose for society

Some Further Rationales for Redistribution

  • Rawls: minimax criterion as natural choice when deciding from ‘behind a veil of ignorance’, and focus on equality of outcomes
  • but: Nozick: society has no command over an income it can redistribute;
    • hence: rather focus on equality of opportunity
    • commodity egalitarianism
    • altruism
    • social stability

three canonical cases obtain for varying degrees of inequality aversion, \(\varepsilon\):

  1. \(\varepsilon=0\): society indifferent to inequality, Bentham or utilitarian
  2. \(0<\varepsilon<\infty\): society averse to inequality, Nash or convex case
  3. \(\varepsilon\to\infty\): society extremely inequality averse, Rawls or maximin case

The actual social welfare function depicted in the graph is due to Anthony Atkinson and reads \[ A_\varepsilon(u_A, u_B)= \begin{cases} 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\left(\frac{1}{2}\left(u_{A}^{1-\varepsilon}+u_{B}^{1-\varepsilon}\right)\right)^{1/(1-\varepsilon)} & \mbox{for}\ 0 \leq \epsilon \neq 1 \\ 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\left(u_A\cdot u_B\right)^{1/2} & \mbox{for}\ \varepsilon=1, \end{cases} \] where \(\varepsilon\) can take any value from 0 upwards and \(\mu\) is a constant.

This flexibility allows to consider a large range of (symmetric and convex) social indifference curves.

Similar to: CES indifference curves for a single consumer.

Interpersonal Comparability and SWF

  • consumer theory: ordinal utility function; \(u\) determined up to any positive monotone transformation
  • ordinal utility consistent with Pareto efficiency as social choice criterion
  • interpersonal comparability meaningless in the ordinal framework
  • instead, allowing for interpersonal comparability helps reflect inequality considerations in a transparent way, and allows constructing an SWF

First Best

  • Combining SWF and UPF allows to determine the optimal social choice: First Best, \(F\).
  • \(F\) may differ when different SWF is applied
  • Technically, UPF acts as a constraint on SWF.
  • This way, we bring together Pareto efficiency (UPF) and society’s preference with respect to equity.

Second Theorem of Welfare Economics

  • Theorem
  • Illustration
  • Notes
  • markets in themselves may generate inequality as a side-effect
  • 1st Theorem: market equilibrium is Pareto efficient; it is silent on equity
    • how about we redistribute when market outcomes not ‘fair’ or ‘too unequal’

Second Theorem of Welfare Economics

Any Pareto efficient outcome can be achieved as a competitive equilibrium, for some distribution of initial endowments.

  • i.o.w.: market clearing \(\impliedby\) efficiency
    • distribution function as additional role of gov’t

Caveat: This needs initial, non-distortionary lump-sum taxes and transfers before market interaction starts.

  • allocations on the UPF are all Pareto efficient, but differ in terms of inequality of utilities (consumptions, incomes)
  • since the most-preferred (SWF-maximizing) allocation is achievable, equity and efficiency are, in fact, separable
  • lump-sum taxes and transfers are a theoretical benchmarking tool, but not realistic

Second Best

  • Lump Sum
  • Okun’s Leaky Bucket
  • Illustration
  • Implications
  • 2nd theorem: ‘more equity’ (equality) involves lump-sum redistribution
  • lump sum: form of taxation that is not tied to individual’s choices (e.g.: income, consumption, wealth), but only to the identity of the tax payer
    • tax payer cannot influence his tax bill
  • reason: do not want substitution effects, as they lead to welfare losses

‘First Theorem of Public Finance’: There is no such thing as a lump-sum tax.

  • i.o.w.: all taxation is distortionary

Consider a redistribution program that takes from the rich and gives to the poor.

[…] the money must be carried from the rich to the poor in a leaky bucket. Some of it will simply disappear in transit, so the poor will not receive all the money that is taken from the rich. […] I want you to decide how much leakage you would accept and still support [this plan]. Suppose 10 percent leaks out […] Should society still make the switch? If 50 percent leaks out? 75 percent? […] Where would you draw the line? Your answer cannot be right or wrong. […] Of course, the leak represents an inefficiency. The inefficiencies of real-world redistribution include the adverse effects on the economic incentives of the rich and the poor, and the administrative costs of tax-collection and transfer programs.
\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\) Arthur M. Okun, 1975,
\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\) Equality and Efficiency: The Big Tradeoff.

  • efficiency losses shift the UPF inwards
  • second best \(S\): maximize social welfare subject to the constraint that efficiency losses are unavoidable
  • trade-off: equity \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) redistribution \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) taxes \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) welfare costs \(\uparrow\)

Implications for the Cost of Public Activity

  • if all taxation is distortionary, then public finance-relevant activities will be associated with the (social) welfare cost of raising taxes

  • these social cost of public funds should be held as small as possible, but are unavoidable

Conclusions

Summary

  • The marginal rate of substitution is a key measure to capture an individual’s willingness to trade (or give up, or pay)
  • Pareto efficiency (equalization of MRS across individuals) is a key measure of societal welfare
  • Pareto efficiency can be illustrated with an Edgeworth box
  • To achieve Pareto efficiency, one can rely on competitive markets (1st Theorem)


  • Pareto efficiency does not account for inequality concerns
  • The social welfare function does allow to reflect inequality aversion
  • The first-best social choice combines the quest for efficiency with views on equity
  • To achieve the first best, lump-sum taxes and transfers are needed (2nd Theorem)
  • Those do not exist, hence distortionary taxation allows at most second best social choice (efficiency-equity tradeoff)
