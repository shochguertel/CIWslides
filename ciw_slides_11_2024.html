11.2 Risk

Risk Attitudes

  • Fair Bet
  • Risk Attitude
  • different people have different attitudes to risk: some try to avoid or shun any risk, others don’t care, others enjoy it
  • risk attitude is a preference attribute, expressed by considering the willingness to accept a fair bet \[ \left. \begin{array}{rl} \text{risk averse:} & \text{unwilling} \\ \text{risk neutral:} & \text{indifferent} \\ \text{risk loving:} & \text{willing} \end{array} \right\} \text{to make a fair bet} \]
  • a fair bet is an uncertain prospect with a known set of possible outcomes and a known set of associated probabilities that sum to one (a pure random event); the expected value of the fair bet is zero
  • consider a risky prospect \(X\) such as income or wealth (not necessarily a fair bet): this is a random variable with realizations \(x\); consider a utility function \(u(x)\), measuring the satisfaction derived from the outcome \(x\)
    • risk attitude is reflected in the shape of the utility function \[ \left. \begin{array}{r} \text{risk averse} \\ \text{risk neutral} \\ \text{risk loving} \end{array} \right\} u \text{ is } \left\{ \begin{array}{l} \text{concave} \\ \text{linear} \\ \text{convex} \end{array} \right. \]
  • for concavity/convexity, the second derivative of the utility function is interesting

Expected Utility, I

  • Definition
  • Risk Attitude

consider a discrete r.v. \(X\) and \(S\) different possible outcomes with associated probabilities \(\pi_s, s=1,\ldots,S\), then the expected value (E.V.) of \(X\) is \[\text E X = \sum_{s=1}^S \pi_s \cdot x_s\] the expected utility is the E.V. of utility \[\text E u(X) = \sum_{s=1}^S \pi_s \cdot u(x_s)\]

\(s\) \(\pi_s\) \(x_s\) \(u(x_s)=\sqrt{x_s}\)

  • expected values are fixed numbers (\(X\) varies, but \(\text E (X)\) or \(\text E u(X)\) does not)
  • risk attitude is reflected in the relation between the expected utility of \(X\) and the utility of \(\text{E}(X)\) \[\begin{array}{rc} \text{risk averse:} & u(\text{E}(X))>\text{E}(u(X)) \\ \text{risk neutral:} & u(\text{E}(X))=\text{E}(u(X)) \\ \text{risk loving:} & u(\text{E}(X))<\text{E}(u(X)) \end{array}\]
  • to a risk averse agent, the utility of the sure thing \(\text E (X)\) is worth more in utility terms than the expected utility of the uncertain prospect \(X\), \(\text{E}(u(X))\)

Expected Utility, II

  • Risk Premium
  • Certainty Equivalent
  • Illustration
  • risk averse agents are willing to pay a `risk premium’ (RP) in order to avoid playing the fair bet
  • risk loving agents have a negative `risk premium’ (they pay to play)
  • risk neutral agents’ RP is zero
  • RP is the difference between \(\text E(X)\) and the certainty-equivalent outcome \(x_c\) (see next tab)
  • the utility at the certainty equivalent outcome \(x_c\), \(u(x_c)\), equals the expected utility of \(X\) \[u(x_c) = \text E u(X)\]
  • apply the inverse transformation on \(u\), \[x_c = u^{-1} (\text E u(X))\]
  • if \(X\) is discrete and all \(S\) values are equally likely \[x_c = u^{-1} \left(\frac{1}{S} \sum_s u(X_s)\right)\]

11.3 Insurance

Insurance

  • Demand for Insurance
  • Contract & Pricing
  • Insurer
  • risk aversion implies there is demand for insurance (positive risk premium)
  • insurance contract: transfers financial risk from insuree to insurer
    • insurance contract: specifies parameters such as
      • premium: price paid by insuree
      • indemnity/coverage: amount insurer pays to insuree in case of loss/damage
      • deductible: insuree’s uninsured amount, ‘own risk’
      • coinsurance: %age of loss borne by the insuree
  • with actuarially fair insurance (‘fair bet’), risk averse agents want to sign insurance contract that obliges them to pay a premium in exchange for having the monetary consequences of a risk fully covered

contract …

  • actuarially fair: premium based purely on probability distribution of insured risk (no administration costs of insurance) = total expected loss divided by number of contracts signed
  • loading: premium also reflects e.g., administration cost of processing insurance claims (load: cost mark-up)
  • full insurance: contract replaces risky prospect \(X\) with its expected value, \(\text{E}(X)\)
    • full insurance covers entire loss, partial insurance does not (indemnity < loss)
  • typically risk neutral
  • signs similar contracts with many people whose individual risks are independent
  • calculates expected loss (possibly including load) as sample average over all contracts
  • under perfect competition in insurance industry, insurer makes zero profit

Insurance Market Problems

  1. macro shocks: if everyone in the insured population gets hit by the same shock, then the funds available for the insurer to pay out will not be sufficient to honor all claims (see US UI claims 3/2020); insurance typically works if insured risks are independent (or at least not strongly positively correlated)
  2. asymmetric information: the insuree knows more about relevant facts than the insurer

public insurance (that cannot go bust) can justify 1., 2. affects all insurers (public or private)

11.4 Asymmetric Information

Two Main Types of Asymmetric Information Problems

  • Health Insurance Example
  • Ex Ante Moral Hazard
  • Ex Post Moral Hazard
  • Adverse Selection

  1. customer chooses his health behavior after signing a contract

    • insurance cover does not provide incentive to take care: ex-ante moral hazard
    • insurance cover incentivizes to choose expensive treatment: ex-post moral hazard

  2. customer knows about his family’s history of diseases before signing a contract

    average premium leads to adverse selection: the good risks choose not to sign the contract

  • empirically, adverse selection and moral hazard hard to distinguish: both imply positive correlation of being insured and experiencing bad health

Adverse Selection Out of Insurance Pooling Contract

  • pooling: same insurance contract offered to all customers
  • competitive insurance markets (many firms) where actuarially fair insurance (based only on expected risk) is offered
  • but: individual probabilities to incur losses differ in the population (different types)
  • informational asymmetry: customer knows his personal probability but insurer only knows distribution of these probabilities
  • individual purchases policy only if premium \(\le\) his expected loss
  • one-size-fits-all premium implies: for some, this is a bad deal
  • low-risk people will not sign the contract (adverse selection)
  • premium\(\uparrow\) \(\quad\to\quad\) reinforces adverse sel. \(\quad\to\quad\) vicious circle

Adverse Selection in Price-Quantity Diagram

Einav & Finkelstein (2011, JEP)

  • every consumer buys or does not buy 1 insurance contract; \(Q\): aggregate # contracts
  • heterogeneity in loss probability: some have higher loss probability than others
  • heterogeneity in risk premium: some are prepared to pay more than others
  • positive correlation between the two: those who have the highest willingness to pay for insurance are those who are expected to be the most costly for the insurer to cover
  • downward sloping demand curve for insurance: the first customers have highest willingness to pay, the last ones zero (or some other, low value)
  • marginal cost \(MC\) curve of insurer downward sloping
  • average cost \(AC\) curve of insurer lies above \(MC\) (also downward sloping)
  • break-even point (equilibrium) insurers: \(D=AC\)

Adverse Selection in Price-Quantity Diagram

  • heterogeneity in risk types at the base of selection:
    • high risk = high MC to insurer
    • high risk with high marginal willingness to pay (MC \(\to\) demand)
  • possibly: heterogeneity in risk attitude (MC not parallel to demand)
  • some may be inefficiently uninsured
  • loads: shift MC, AC, not demand
  • some may be efficiently uninsured
  • mandate can flip this around

Market Unravelling in Price-Quantity Diagram

  • complete market unravelling (\(Q_{\text{eq}} \to 0\)) possible (‘’The Death Spiral of Adverse Selection’’)
  • extreme outcome: no insurance in market equilibrium at all, Akerlof’s (1970) lemon principle
  • depends on exact location of \(MC\) and \(D\)

Moral Hazard

  • assume no (adverse) selection (no heterogeneity in risk probabilities)
    • MC and AC curves are flat and identical
  • MC\(_{unins}\) shows the MC (AC) curve for the case that consumers are uninsured, MC\(_{ins}\) is for the same customers that are insured
    • uniform increase in risk probability due to being insured
    • vertical difference measures equilibrium price response to moral hazard

Advantageous Selection

  • assume absence of moral hazard
  • strongly negative correlation between risk aversion and risk (the most risk averse people with the lowest cost to the insurer are the ones with the highest willingness to pay), MC slopes upward
  • may lead to advantageous selection and overinsurance

Policy Options

  • loading: admin costs shift up MC & AC without shifting demand
    • despite risk aversion, some people don’t want insurance

Mandate

  • a mandate breaks the vicious death spiral of adverse selection
    • can lead to lower prices, and higher consumer surplus
  • it makes those worse off that are forced to pay more than what they value it for
    • countervailing effect on welfare loss
    • need SWF to weigh losers and gainers

Subsidy

  • a subsidy can restore the effect of loading
    • similar to: approach of Pigou taxes/subsidies

Conclusions

Social Insurance Mandate

  • Mandate
  • Adv Selection
  • Solidarity
  • Commitment

A mandate (obligation to participate) is used in social insurances for the following reasons:

  • combat adverse selection
  • equality of access
  • enforce solidarity
    • risk solidarity
    • income solidarity
  • combat commitment problems
  • see the previous analysis
    • mandates break the death spiral of adverse selection, leading to lower prices
    • increasing affordability & equality of access
    • may force people into insurance that don’t want it
  • trade-off: use SWF to choose
  • efficient pricing may entail pricing different risks differentially
  • mandated insurance can do without efficient pricing
Risk Solidarity
  • abandons or mitigates differentiation so that the worse risks are not confronted with the full cost they cause
  • may forego moral-hazard-limiting benefits of co-insurance or deductible
Income Solidarity
  • links premiums paid to income, without risk-related reason
  • mandated system helps to redistribute outside the tax system (vertical equity)
  • psychological aspect: many people cannot effectively commit to a good plan of action that is in their own best interest
    • e.g. voluntary retirement savings
  • mandated participation makes non-commitment a non-issue

Summary

  • expected utility theory is standard economics approach to model rational decisionmaking under risk
    • known and complete set of outcomes
    • known (or presumed) set of probabilities
  • risk aversion as canonical preference description, positively associated with
    • concavity of utility (\(u''<0\))
    • risk premium
  • risk averse individuals have demand for insurance
  • insurance redistributes from the lucky to the not-so-lucky
  • prime insurance market failure surface under asymmetric information
    • adverse selection (in the extreme: ‘death spiral’)
    • moral hazard
  • social insurances use mandates to break adverse selection, to enforce solidarity, and to protect people from themselves
