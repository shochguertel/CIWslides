5 Technology, Externalities, and Public Goods

5.1 Technology

Firm’s Production Technology

  • Production Function
  • Production Possibilities
  • Allocative Efficiency
  • upper row: a firm’s production technology relates input quantity \(z\) to maximally feasible output quantity \(q=f(z)\)

  • lower row: the derivative is the marginal product or marginal productivity
  • from left to right decreasing returns to scale / linear / increasing returns to scale
  • left panel:
    • with two inputs \(q=f(z_1, z_2=\text{low})\) and \(q=f(z_1, z_2=\text{high})\) (dotted)
  • the production possibilities frontier (PPF) shows what an economy can maximally produce in terms of multiple outputs (say, \(x\) and \(y\)) when the amount of available input is limited to \(\bar z\), and a choice needs to be made whether to use a given input unit in \(x\) or in \(y\): \(z_x\) or \(z_y\)
  • the slope of the PPF is called the marginal rate of transformation, MRT
  • if production possibilities determine what can physically be produced, we can imagine that the PPF limits the availability of resources \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\) that determine the size of an Edgeworth box
  • The Edgeworth box illustrates the condition for Pareto Efficiency: \(MRS_A = MRS_B\)
  • allocative efficiency is achieved where \(MRS_A = MRS_B = MRT\), i.e., the marginal willingness to give up \(y\) for \(x\) of consumers corresponds to producers’ marginal transformation of \(y\) into \(x\)

If consumers want to trade 3 apples for 2 bananas (\(MRS\)), but producers can produce 3 extra bananas from giving up production of 3 apples (\(MRT\)), then the economy could get 1 extra banana from the same underlying resources. Obviously, the condition for allocative efficiency has not been met.

5.2 Externalities

Market Failure

  • the model so far implicitly excluded two important reasons for market failure
    • externalities
    • public goods
  • both have in common that the price mechanism does not work well because the benefits or the cost do not accrue to the responsible party
  • at the root of it are ill-defined or undefined property rights (who has the right to clean air? who owns cybersecurity or protection from a pandemic?)

  • we can characterize conditions for the optimal allocation to find:
    • public goods and goods with positive externalities tend to be underprovided
    • goods with negative externalities tend to be overprovided
  • those aspects point to allocative failure and non-maximized welfare
  • the allocative efficiency condition \(MRS = MRT\) will typically be violated

Externalities Illustrated

  • Definition
  • Graph

Externality

effect of own economic activity on somebody else’s without compensation payments that are accounted for in market prices

  • externalities are essentially unintended spillover effects
  • affect non-market participants (or `bystanders’)
  • externalities can
    • be negative, positive, and/or interdependent
    • affect production and/or consumption
  • in what follows, focus on pollution: firm pollutes while producing, consumers (as bystanders) suffer marginal damages from the pollution—which is assumed to be increasing in the level of production
  • a rational private actor will not take into account social cost/benefits, their own private cost/benefits
  • in market equilibrium: \[\text{marg. private cost} = \text{marg. private benefits}\] will have too much of a negative externality
  • policy solutions try to restore optimal allocation: \[\text{marg. social cost} = \text{marg. social benefits}\]
  • this does not imply: abolish all activity with externalities; even at optimal allocation there will be some level of externality present

Pollution Abatement Strategies

  • financial incentives through the tax system
    • tax the externality-causing production
    • subsidize abatement (= reduction of pollution)

  • tackle emission directly
    • command-and-control quantity regulation
    • cap-and-trade pollution permits
    • emissions fee (tax savings on abatement)

Policy: Pigou Tax

  • Idea
  • Graph

Pigovian Tax

Tax per unit on a polluting producer’s output equal to the marginal damage it causes at the socially optimal level of production. Concept named after A.C. Pigou (1877–1959).

  • i.e., introduce a price on the externality that the polluter has to pay (thus make the tax part of private marginal cost)
  • this will align marginal social cost of society with marginal private cost of the polluter

  • main problem: implementation
    • get polluters and pollutees to truthfully reveal their private cost and benefits so that taxes can be set optimally
  • Tax increases MPC such that polluters reduce their economic activity
    • Optimal tax reduces activity such that the welfare loss from the externality is exactly removed
  • Subsidy on abatement likewise increases MPC when paid on pollution reduction exceeding a prespecified benchmark; here, the marginal value of not abating carries a cost of foregone subsidy
    • Optimal subsidy increases abatement such that the welfare loss from the externality is exactly removed

Get the hang of Pigovian Taxes!

  • Choose tax or subsidy and then increase the rate to the optimum.

Aside: Optimal Cost Allocation Decision Between 2 Firms

Consider two firms \(f\) and \(g\) with different (marginal) cost functions

  • limited output target \(\bar q\) in industry
  • increasing marginal cost for \(f\) and \(g\) (reversed: from right to left)
  • inefficient allocation: \(MC_f\neq MC_g\)
  • efficient allocation: \(MC_f = MC_g\) (min. total cost)

Policy: Quantity Regulation

  • gov’t directly controls quantity of production or abatement
  • one-size-fits-all inefficient with differentiated firms
    • how much does the gov’t know about firms’ cost?

Play!

  • in the example, it happens that the optimal split between firms is 50:50
  • shift the black dot on the right axis to change the total \(\bar q\), say from 6 to 4
  • if regulation restricts production, then a reallocation across firms is desirable
  • a continued 50:50 split is undesirable
  • market-based solutions give firms incentives to change their production decisions optimally, given the changed policy parameters
  • this can be achieved through a Pigovian tax or through a cap-and-trade solution

Policy: Cap-and-Trade

  • A cap-and-trade system relies on establishing a market for pollution, by issuing and allocating emission rights or pollution permits
  • the regulator chooses a quantity \(\bar q\) of such permits and an initial allocation (say, 50:50), the cap
  • conditional on this “endowment” the two firms engage in mutual trade to find the optimal allocation: the firm with the lower marginal cost to abate can sell some of its permits to the high-cost firm, until marginal costs are equalized across firms
  • over time, the regulator then buys back some permits, reducing \(\bar q\) and overall pollution
  • in bigger economies, this can be organized as an auction or a stock exchange
  • implemented as EU-ETS see NEA infographic

Policy: Emissions Fee

  • similar to a Pigovian tax that is levied on output, the emissions fee is a unit tax directly levied on pollution and hence introduces a price on polluting
  • to avoid the tax, a firm abates until its marginal cost of abatement equals the level of the fee (so it pays no tax for the achieved pollution reduction), after which it rather pays the fee but does not abate anymore
  • similar to the emission permits market, cost efficiency is achieved between firms since all firms adjust their pollution decision given the price of pollution
  • those for whom it is cheapest abate most, high polluters pay highest tax bills
  • note: during inflationary periods, need upward tax rate changes

Samuelson: Optimal Provision of Public Goods

  • Vertical Aggregation
  • Allocative Efficiency

  • demand aggregated across consumers is
    • private good: horizontal
    • public good: vertical sum of individual demand func’s
  • for two individuals \(A\) and \(B\)
    • private good \(x\) \[\mathit{MC}_{x} = \mathit{MB}_{x}^A = \mathit{MB}_{x}^B\]
    • public good \(G\) \[\mathit{MC}_{G} = \mathit{MB}_{G}^A + \mathit{MB}_{G}^B\]
  • the latter is the simplified form of the Samuelson condition

Samuelson Condition with a private good \(x\) and a public good \(G\):

\[MRS_A + MRS_B = MRT\]

recall:

  • MRS – willingness to give up some \(x\) when getting 1 marginal unit of \(G\) (slope of indifference curve)
  • MRT – units of \(x\) to relinquish when needing to produce 1 extra unit of \(G\) (slope of production possibilities frontier)
  • private goods: \(MRS_A = MRS_B = MRT\)

Market Outcome: Prisoner’s Dilemma (PD)

payoffs: abating costs 7 to the abater, and brings benefits of 5 to every player including oneself

  • the PD is a non-cooperative game where each player maximizes their own payoff
  • two rational countries will not by themselves adopt tighter pollution standards (‘pollute’ is dominant strategy for each)
  • despite there being gains from doing so, collectively
  • cooperation would have led to Pareto improvement:
    • play { abate, abate } and share total gain of \(3+3=6\) among both players (\(3\) each)
  • assume here: no commitment possible

Global Public Goods (GPGs)

  • Issue
  • Examples
  • Covid19
  • Typology
  • as every PG, GPGs are non-rival and non-excludable
  • but their range is global (or at least transnational)
  • once provided, no country can be prevented from enjoying a GPG; nor can any country’s enjoyment of the good impinge on the consumption opportunities of other countries
  • if provided, GPGs make citizens better off everywhere
  • typically, some sort of coordination between countries is required
Including Global Public Bads and Impure Versions

  • common pool resources (e.g., overfishing)

  • Single Best Effort

every country would be willing to sacrifice resources for provision and go it alone; one country is needed to provide it once and for all
Ex.: asteroid annihilating planet earth

  • Weakest Link

can only be provided with the active participation of every country provision depends on weakest individual effort
Ex.: smallpox eradication

  • Aggregate Effort

provision of the good depends on total effort of many countries
Ex.: reduction of GHG emissions

  • Mutual Restraint

countries should refrain from doing the wrong thing
Ex.: nuclear disarmament

  • Coordination

countries do the same thing, or coordinate their actions
Ex.: standards of time, weighs and measures

Unilateral Action v Cooperation

  • Issue
  • Welfare Loss Illustrated
  • unilateral action, where players forego the benefits from cooperation and do not care about others free-riding on them, can help some:
  • if good’s quantity is binary (annihilation example: all or nothing), in a single-best-effort provision there is no free-riding problem, since the good will not be underprovided
  • more generally, might observe this where a player has private marginal benefits exceed marginal cost of provision
  • if the good’s quantity is continous, still have free-riding and underprovision
  • welfare loss (compared to full cooperation) remains but is smaller (compared to non-cooperation)
  • efficient solution Samuelson rule: \(G^*\)
  • private-market equilibrium corresponding to Prisoner’s Dilemma is at a quantity of \(G=0\)
    • welfare loss (total surplus foregone) is large
  • unilateral action by player \(j\): choose quantity \(G^o\) such that \[MB_j(G^o) = MC(G^o)\]
    • welfare loss is smaller

Pollution Abatement Game with \(N\) Players

  • Notation
  • Non-cooperative Play
  • Cooperative Approach
  • symmetric \(N\)-player game with 2 actions (binary): abate or pollute
  • denote player \(i\)’s binary abatement action \(q_i = \{ 0, 1 \}\) for (pollute, abate)
  • denote total abatement by all players \[Q=\sum_{j=1}^N q_j = q_i+Q_{-i};\qquad Q_{-i} \equiv \sum_{j\neq i; j=1}^N q_j\]
  • benefit \(b\): everyone’s abatement benefits everybody (all players)
  • cost \(c\): cost of a player’s abatement is private
  • payoff for player \(i\): benefit minus cost, \[\Pi_i(q_i)= b\cdot Q - c \cdot q_i = (b-c) \cdot q_i + b \cdot Q_{-i} \]
  • note: \(Q\) [\(Q_{-1}\)] is equal to the total number of [other] players playing ‘abate’
  • aka market solution
  • everybody maximizes their own payoff, taking other players’ choices as given \[\max_{q_i} \Pi_i(q_i)\]
  • social planner simultaneously maximizes collective payoff of all players
  • for instance, the sum of all payoffs \(\Pi(Q) = \sum_i^N \Pi_i(q_i)\) \[\max_{Q} \Pi(Q) \qquad \to \quad Q^\star\]
  • and share the result (evenly in a symmetric game) \[\Pi_i^\star = \frac{1}{N} \Pi(Q^\star)\]


  • for a subgroup: share the result when only \(k<N\) players want to cooperate (divide the total gain by \(k\))

Treaties

  • Issue
  • Data
  • Upshot
  • consider possibility of acceding voluntary int’l agreements (treaties)
  • most international agreements are based on the idea to have a voluntary club of countries that commits to some form of cooperative action
  • joiners can, like players in an Edgeworth box, find a Pareto efficient solution for themselves and harness the gains from cooperation
  • however, many countries would not want to join, since free-riding is more beneficial than cooperating

Selfenforcing Agreements

Treaties must be self-enforcing, and do more than simply tell countries what to do. Treaties must make it in the own interest of countries to voluntarily behave as every country would like them to behave.

Treaty Solution to Global Public Goods

  • Idea
  • Illustration
  • Solution
  • Role of \(N\)
  • exploit the gains from cooperation: two (generally: \(k\)) countries could simply decide to commit to ‘abate’, then share the collective gains among participants
  • since participation is voluntary, each such country compares the net benefit from cooperation with the net benefit from free-riding


Three stages of a Treaty:

  1. non-cooperative: each country decides unilaterally whether or not to join / sign the treaty
    • benefit from joining: joint payoff from cooperation \(>\) payoff from not-cooperating
    • benefit from leaving: free-riding on others (benefit from others’ effort, no cost to oneself)
  2. cooperative: the \(k\) signatories to the treaty ‘abate’ and share the gains from cooperation
  3. non-cooperative: the \(N-k\) non-signatories decide unilaterally whether to ‘abate’ or ‘pollute’

Solution is backward: start with 3.

3. any non-signatory will ‘pollute’, since no matter what, this is the dominant strategy in non-cooperative play

2. determine the payoff from cooperating, conditional on \(k\)

1. determine \(k\) by asking per individual player: is it worth to join and ‘abate’, or are the payoffs from staying out and ‘polluting’ higher?

  • it can be shown that the equilibrium number of treaty participants, \(k^\star\), is driven by ratio of \(c\) to \(b\); \(k^\star\) is an integer: \[ \frac{c}{b}\le k^\star \le \frac{c}{b}+1\]

  • example: \(c=7\), \(b=5\) \(\quad\Rightarrow\quad\) \(k^\star =2\)

  • equilibrium number of signatories, typically \(k^\star\ll N\)

  • symmetry: the approach may be able to predict how large the groups are, \(k^\star\) and \(N-k^\star\), but will not say anything about the identity of players

  • \(N\) large and large gains of cooperation entails large incentive to leave
    • by leaving, the leaver suffers little in terms of reduced benefits, but gains a lot in terms of reduced costs
    • leaving is very attractive
  • \(N\) very large: treaties can achieve very little
    • treaties tend to codify what is already happening (smallest common denominator)
    • requiring drastic changes will put off potential members
  • \(N\) small:
    • credible punishments will be large relative to the gains from leaving, and a self-enforcing agreement can sustain a high participation rate

Conclusion

Summary

  • externalities and public goods are prime sources of market failure: they will not be optimally provided by interaction of self-interested market players, either too much (negative externalities), or too little (positive externalities, public goods)
  • we can characterize optimality conditions for an efficient allocation for externalities and public goods
    • externality: marginal social cost = marginal social benefit
    • public good: marginal cost = sum of marginal benefits (or, MRT = \(\sum\)MRS)
  • gov’t intervention or coordination can help
    • Pigou / carbon taxes / emission fees
    • cap and trade on markets for pollution/emission rights (ETS)
    • coordination for sub-national PGs by the national gov’t
  • for Global PG’s, where no world gov’t can intervene, treaty solutions may improve on uncoordinated action, but may not reach the optimal level of provision
    • players that agree to the treaty are obliged to cooperate and share the gains
    • the decision to join or leave the treaty is voluntary
    • free-riding incentive limits incentive to cooperate
