3.1 Inequality

Inequality of Income, Consumption or Wealth

  • to quantify inequality, often one uses microdata on (household) income
  • this could be survey or administrative data
    • since the introduction of the income tax, about 100 years ago, governments know who reports how much income, and they keep administrative registers
  • consumption is only available from survey data, and often preferred in the context of developing countries
    • smallholder farmers (consumer-producers) may consume what they produce, their market income may underestimate their welfare levels
    • self-employment is partly associated with income tax evasion
    • consumption tends to fluctuate less as households engage in consumption smoothing over time, and across members of the family or household
  • recent attention to wealth inequality, as microeconomic survey data became available, but since many countries do not have a comprehensive wealth tax, reliable measures of wealth at the micro level are scarce

Statistical Distributions

  • PDF, CDF
  • Graph
  • measuring inequality is about describing and quantifying inequality of some distribution of a variable of interest (income, wealth, consumption) in statistical terms
  • let \(Y\) denote the variable of interest
  • main idea: take the values \(Y\) (or a random sample) as given and study the aspects of its distribution that tell how `different’ people are from one another
  • if \(Y\) is continuously distributed, then its distribution can be represented by
    • a probability density function (pdf) \(f_Y(y)\), or
    • a cumulative distribution function (cdf) \(F_Y(y)=\int_{\min\{y\}}^y f(z) \text{d}z\)

Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient

  • Description
  • Illustration
  • Alternatives\(^\star\)
  • most popular measure for inequality are Gini coefficient and Lorenz curve \(\Lambda\)
  • \(\Lambda\) encompasses all values of the distribution and can be derived from the pdf or cdf

We shall derive \(\Lambda\) graphically in class.

  • starting point are the values of \(Y\) in ascending order
  • determine \(p\), the cumulative percentile of individuals considered (runs from 0 to 100%)
  • \(\Lambda\) measures for each \(p\) the cumulative share of income in total income (runs from 0 to 100%)
  • \(\Lambda\) is typically below the 45\(^\circ\)line, monotonically increasing and convex
  • Gini coefficient of inequality is based on the cumulative difference between the line of equality and the Lorenz curve

There are many different statistical summary measures of inequality. The following are examples of measures conforming to the

Pigou-Dalton Principle

i.e., under a transfer from a rich to a poor individual that preserves both ranking of individuals and total income, inequality must decrease.

\[\begin{aligned} \mbox{CV}^x &=& \frac{\sigma}{\mu\sqrt{N-1}} \\ \mbox{Gini} &=& \frac{1}{2N^2\mu} \sum_i \sum_j | y_i-y_j| \end{aligned}\]

\[\begin{aligned} \mbox{Theil} &=& \frac{1}{N \ln N} \sum \frac{y_i}{\mu} \ln \frac{y_i}{\mu} \\ ^\dagger\mbox{Atkinson}_\varepsilon &=& 1 - \left[\frac{1}{N} \sum \left( \frac{y_i}{\mu} \right)^{1-\varepsilon} \right]^{\frac{1}{1-\varepsilon}} \end{aligned}\]

\(^x\)CV is the coefficient of variation (altogether different from the concept of compensating variation) \(^\dagger\varepsilon\) is a policy parameter that expresses society’s degree of inequality aversion

Inequality and Policy

  • Redistribution
  • Illustration
  • Notes
  • If SWF suggests that inequality is of policy concern, then redistribution, typically from the rich to the poor, is a possible choice
  • note: such redistribution does not tackle the causes of inequality, rather its symptoms
  • distribution of incomes \(\{y_1,\ldots,y_N\}\)
  • Budget-neutral redistribution: tax everyone using proportional income tax \(\tau\), redistribute total tax revenue \(\sum_i \tau y_i\) as a lump sum to everyone: \[ \mbox{after-tax income } \tilde{y}_i = y_i(1-\tau)+\frac{\tau \sum_i y_i}{N} \]
  • assume mean income does not change with redistribution (see notes)\(^\dagger\)

We shall show the effect of redistribution on \(\Lambda\) graphically in class.

  • redistribution shifts the \(\Lambda\) curve towards the line of equality and reduces the Gini
  • Atkinson has shown that social welfare, as measured through any concave SWF, increases with such redistribution (see notes)\(^\ddagger\)
  • \(^\dagger\) ideally, one should take into account behavioral effects of redistributional taxation, that is, redistribution may be associated with (possibly large) negative effects on labor supply and earnings; in that case, society “buys” more equality with “less efficiency” and lower mean income (see the equity/efficiency trade-off, and the Second Best)
  • \(^\ddagger\) Lorenz curves such as \(\Lambda\) and \(\Lambda'\) curves might cross one another; in that case, it is unclear from those curves alone which distribution has more inequality

3.2 Poverty

Poverty

  • Definition
  • Aim of Measurement
  • Policies

Poverty, specifically

  • lack of command over commodities
    • “… a severe constriction of the choice set [over commodities]” (Harold Watts)
  • Lack of specific consumptions in terms of quantity or quality
    • e.g. too little food energy intake, too little of specific nutrients, leaking roof, …

Poverty, more generally

  • lack of “welfare” in wider sense
    • e.g. lack of “capability”: inability to achieve certain “functionings”
    • “beings and doings” (Amartya Sen)
  • supply evidence / quantitative data to inform policy on the extent, developments/trends, and severity of poverty
  • keep poor people on the political agenda
  • Growth strategy
    • Has poverty increased? Did growth help the poor? How did relative price changesaffect the poor? What is the impact of food price fluctuations? Who were the losers and who were the gainersfrom economy-wide policy reforms?
  • Social Spending
    • Who uses public services? At what cost? Who benefits from government subsidies? On average, versus at the margin? Who will be hurt by retrenchment?
  • Targeted Interventions
    • Who are the target groups? How should transfers be allocated? How much impact will they have on poverty?

Canonical Poverty Measures

  • Measures
  • Graph
  • Math\(^\star\)

Canonical statistical measures of poverty (Poverty Indices, PI)

  • headcount ratio (HC): what percentage of population has income below poverty line?
    • easy to measure and understand
  • poverty gap (PG): what is the average relative distance of the poor from the poverty line?
    • reflects (average) depth of poverty
  • squared PG (SPG): what is the average relative squared distance of the poor from the poverty line?
    • reflects severity of poverty, as it gives the highest weight to the poorest
  • the graph shows the three canonical measures
  • for a mathematical expression check the Math tab

Get the hang of poverty measures!

  • shift the poverty line to see how poverty measures change
  • click the links to see the implicit weighting scheme

The Foster-Greer-Thorbecke (FGT) index captures all three PIs \[ FGT_{\alpha }={\frac {1}{N}}\sum _{i=1}^{H}\left({\frac {z-y_{i}}{z}}\right)^{\alpha } \]

  • \(y_i\) individual income, \(i=1,\ldots,N\)
  • \(z\) poverty line
  • \(N\) number of people
  • \(H\) number of poor (incomes below \(z\))
  • \(\alpha\): definitional parameter, \(\in \{0,1,2\}\)
  • \(\alpha=0\): HC: \(\displaystyle FGT_{0} = \frac{H}{N}\)
  • \(\alpha=1\): PG: \(\displaystyle FGT_{1} = {\frac {1}{N}}\sum _{i=1}^{H}\left({\frac {z-y_{i}}{z}}\right) = FGT_{0} \cdot \bar{y}_p\)
    • \(\bar{y}_p\) avg income of the poor
  • \(\alpha=2\): SPG: \(\displaystyle FGT_{2} = {\frac {1}{N}}\sum _{i=1}^{H}\left({\frac {z-y_{i}}{z}}\right)^{2}\)

Poverty Relief Measures and Consumer Choice

  • Tools
  • In-Kind
  • CV
  • transfers are used to address poverty to targeted and identified individuals
    • in cash or in kind
  • cash transfers can be used to
    • move individuals back to the poverty line in terms of disposable income
      • e.g., in inflationary periods
    • counteract a price hike on specific goods
      • e.g., using the idea of compensating variation
  • in-kind transfers for particular expenditure categories (necessitities)
    • e.g., social/council housing; food stamps/coupons/special debit cards; reduced public transport fares; access to health services
    • in-kind transfers reduce the choice set

  • in-kind benefit is tied to consumption of good \(x_1\)
  • it cannot be (directly) exchanged for good \(x_2\)
    • there is a kink at \(E_2\) on the red BC
    • individual might choose to consume at the kink
  • cash benefit is fully fungible (grey BC), there is no kink, and it might afford higher utility at \(E_3\)
  • CV restores welfare to the level of utility that was obtained before the price hike
  • this involves a money transfer to recipients
  • the size of the transfer is meant not to make recipients better off than they had been before the price hike
  • instead it corresponds to the cheapest compensation from the point of view of the government that leaves the recipient indifferent to experiencing and not experiencing the price hike

Anti-Poverty Policy and Labor Supply

  • Tools
  • Minimum Wage
  • UBI
  • Means Test
  • EITC
  • citizens need to be encouraged to earn their own income and be self-reliant
  • enabling people to earn a living wage is part of the objective

We look at the following policy tools:

  • minimum wage (floor on hourly wages, irrespective of skill level or experience)
  • unconditional basic income (lump-sum payment to all, no strings attached)
  • means-tested benefits (benefits exceeding own earnings up to the poverty line)
  • earned-income tax credit (targeted wage subsidy, turning stepwise into tax)

Policy can have unintended consequences (e.g., through perverse incentives)

  • minimum wage has, as any other wage rate \(w\) change two effects on labor supply
    • an income effect
    • a substitution effect
  • there is the possibility that at some level of \(w\) labor supply decreases

  • Universal Basic Income is an unconditional fixed benefit
    • possibily while removing all other conditional transfers
  • UBI shifts the budget constraint up
    • there may be income effects on labor supply, whose sign depends on indifference curve
    • no substitution effects
  • in reality: UBI is almost never truly universal, but there are only few experiments (e.g. Finland)

  • benefit \(b\) should bring the individual to the poverty line \(y_{\min}\)
  • but will be cut accordingly as recipient’s own means (earnings, wealth) rise
  • implies an implicit tax rate of 100%
    • note the 2 kinks in the BC, expecially at \(E_2\) where people might stick
    • raises the possibility of induced non-participation, and subsequently a poverty trap (work does not pay enough)

  • Earned income tax credit has three phases
  1. phase-in: wage subsidy increases \(w\)
  2. flat part: wage subsidy ceased
  3. phase-out: wage tax decreases \(w\)
  • there are 3 kinks, the first one is possibly most salient
  • keeps incentive to work, at least a small number of hours

Conclusions

Summary

  • Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient as standard statistical measure for inequality assessment
  • redistribution is welfare-enhancing under a convex SWF, and can be achieved using a simple budget-neutral tax-benefit system (assuming no behavioral response)
  • poverty policy aims at providing a consumption floor
  • benefit design can dis-/encourage labor supply (participation)
  • in-kind provision: targeting and self-selection of the needy
  • unintended side effect of means testing: discourage effort taking
  • in a development context, poverty reduction is often eased by strong growth
  • poverty profiling aims to identify who is poor for better targeting (\(\to\)tutorial)
